📊 Understanding Relative Stock Performance

This graph compares how different stocks perform relative to each other by normalizing their starting points to 1.0 (100%). This lets you compare growth rates regardless of actual stock prices.

🔍 Key Features
  • 📈 Normalized Baseline: All stocks start at 1.0 (100%) for fair comparison
  • 🔼 Above 1.0: Stock has gained value (1.25 = 25% profit)
  • 🔽 Below 1.0: Stock has lost value (0.85 = 15% loss)
  • ⏱️ Steepness: Shows speed of growth/decline
📌 Quick Interpretation Guide
Look for:
✔ Highest line → Best performer
✔ Steepest rising line → Strongest growth
✔ Falling lines → Underperforming stocks
✔ Converging lines → Sector-wide trend developing
✔ Diverging lines → Stock-specific events
✔ Sudden spikes/drops → Earnings reports or market shocks
✔ Consistent outperformance → Competitive advantage
👆 Interactive: Hover over points for details

📉 Understanding Daily Returns Distribution

This graph shows the percentage change in stock prices each day. It helps you track how different stocks perform on a daily basis—whether they went up or down in value, and by how much. You can visualize how volatile a stock is and whether it tends to gain or lose value regularly.

🔍 Key Features
  • 📅 Daily View: Each group of bars represents one day, showing how each stock moved on that date.
  • 📊 Colored Bars: Each stock has a different color, making it easy to compare them visually.
  • 🔼 Upward Bar: The stock gained value that day.
  • 🔽 Downward Bar: The stock lost value that day.
  • 👆 Interactive: Hover over points for details
📌 Quick Interpretation Guide
Look for:
✔ Tall upward bars → Stock had a great day
✔ Tall downward bars or short bars in aggregated datasets → Stock had a poor day
✔ Compare colors → See which stock outperformed or underperformed on a given day
✔ Patterns over time → Spot trends, volatility, or consistent growth/decline

⚖️ Understanding Risk vs. Reward Analysis

This scatter plot helps you evaluate the trade-off between risk and reward for different stocks. Each point represents a stock positioned based on its historical volatility (risk) and average returns (reward).

📊 Quadrant Analysis
  • Top-right: High risk, high reward
  • Top-left: Low risk, high reward (ideal)
  • Bottom-right: High risk, low reward (avoid)
  • Bottom-left: Low risk, low reward
📌 Quick Interpretation Guide
How to analyze:
✔ Top-left quadrant → Best investments (high return, low risk)
✔ Diagonal line → Stocks following risk-reward tradeoff
✔ Outliers → Stocks behaving differently from the norm
✔ Point distance → Further apart means more performance variation
👆 Interactive: Hover over points for details
📈 Advanced Insights
Pro Tip: Compare to the market benchmark (S&P 500 typically at ~0.01 daily return, ~0.012 std dev). Stocks significantly above the benchmark's risk-reward ratio may indicate:
• 🚀 Exceptional growth potential
• ⚠️ Potential overvaluation or instability
• 📉 Higher probability of large swings
💡 Remember:

• Past performance ≠ future results

• Higher risk means bigger potential swings

• Diversification can help balance your portfolio

🔗 Understanding Stock Correlation Heatmaps

This heatmap visualizes the correlation between different stocks. Each cell represents the strength and direction of the relationship between a pair of stocks, based on their historical price movements.

📊 Interpreting Correlation Values
  • +1.00: Perfect positive correlation (move together)
  • 0.00: No correlation (movement is unrelated)
  • -1.00: Perfect negative correlation (move in opposite directions)
📌 Quick Analysis Guide
How to analyze:
✔ Darker red → Strong negative correlation
✔ Darker blue → Strong positive correlation
✔ Light colors → Weak or no correlation
✔ Diagonal cells → Self-correlation (always 1.00)
👆 Interactive: Hover or click on a cell to view the exact value
📈 Strategic Insights
Pro Tip: Use correlation analysis to:
• 🔀 Identify diversification opportunities
• 📊 Avoid overexposure to similar market factors
• 🔎 Spot tightly coupled stocks for pair trading strategies
💡 Remember:

• Correlation is not causation

• Relationships can shift with changing market conditions

📶 Understanding Relative Strength Index (RSI)

This chart compares the RSI values across multiple stocks, showing momentum and potential overbought/oversold conditions. RSI measures the speed and change of price movements on a scale of 0 to 100. The 14 day period setting (default for RSI) represents 2 market weeks of trading data. It strikes a balance between sensitivity and reliability.

📊 Key RSI Levels
  • 70+: Overbought territory (potential sell signal)
  • 30-: Oversold territory (potential buy signal)
  • 50: Centerline (bullish above, bearish below)
📌 RSI Interpretation Guide
How to analyze:
✔ Red zone (70+) → Overextended rally, possible reversal
✔ Green zone (30-) → Oversold conditions, potential bounce
✔ Crossing 50 → Momentum shift (bullish/bearish)
✔ Divergences → When price and RSI move opposite directions
👆 Interactive: Hover to see exact values and compare trends
📈 Advanced RSI Strategies
Pro Tip: Use RSI analysis to:
• 🔄 Identify trend reversals early
• ⚖️ Spot overbought/oversold conditions in context
• 🔎 Find bullish/bearish divergences (price vs RSI)
• 📉 Combine with support/resistance levels for confirmation
• 🕰️ Use different timeframes (daily/weekly) for perspective (upcoming feature)
⚠️ RSI Limitations
Important Notes:
• RSI works best in ranging markets (less effective in strong trends)
• Overbought doesn't always mean "sell" (in strong uptrends)
• Oversold doesn't always mean "buy" (in strong downtrends)
💡 Expert Insight:

• Look for RSI failure swings at extremes (70+/30-)

• Combine with volume analysis for stronger signals

• Watch for centerline (50) crosses as confirmation

📊 Understanding Bollinger Bands

This chart displays Bollinger Bands, a volatility indicator that shows how prices are dispersed around an average value. The bands expand and contract based on market volatility, creating a dynamic trading range. The default setting (20 periods, 2 standard deviations) captures approximately 95% of price action within the bands during normal market conditions.

📈 Key Bollinger Band Components
  • Upper Band: 20-period moving average + (2 × standard deviation)
  • Middle Band: 20-period simple moving average (price baseline)
  • Lower Band: 20-period moving average - (2 × standard deviation)
  • Band Width: Measures volatility (wider = more volatile)
📌 Bollinger Bands Interpretation Guide
How to analyze:
✔ Price touches upper band → Potential overbought condition
✔ Price touches lower band → Potential oversold condition
✔ Bands squeeze → Low volatility, often precedes big moves
✔ Bands expand → High volatility, trend continuation likely
✔ Price outside bands → Extreme move, possible reversal
👆 Interactive: Hover to see exact values and band width changes
📉 Advanced Bollinger Band Strategies
Pro Tip: Use Bollinger Bands to:
• 🔄 Identify potential reversal points at band extremes
• 🎯 Spot continuation patterns during band expansions
• 🔍 Recognize volatility contractions before breakouts
• 📊 Combine with %b indicator for precise positioning
• 🕰️ Use with other indicators (like RSI) for confirmation
• 📉 Watch for M-Tops and W-Bottoms pattern formations
⚠️ Bollinger Bands Limitations
Important Notes:
• Works best in ranging markets (can give false signals in trends)
• Touching a band doesn't guarantee reversal (strong trends may ride bands)
• Requires confirmation from other indicators for best results
• Band width alone doesn't indicate direction of next move
💡 Expert Insight:

• The most powerful signals come after band squeezes

• Price tends to return to the middle band over time

• Watch for multiple touches of one band without reversal

• Combine with volume spikes for breakout confirmation

🔧 Customization Options:

• Adjust period length (default 20) for sensitivity

• Modify standard deviations (default 2) for band width

• Try 1.5 deviations for more signals, 2.5 for fewer but stronger signals
